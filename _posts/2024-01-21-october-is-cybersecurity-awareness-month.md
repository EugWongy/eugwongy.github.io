Improve your cybersecurity by taking 4 simple steps this Cyber Security Awareness Month.

1. Update your devices regularly
2. Turn on multi- factor authentication (MFA)
3. Back up your important files
4. Use passphrases and password managers

---

Be cyber wise- don't compromise- that’s the theme this year for Cybersecurity Awareness Month. The Australian Cyber Security Center encourages everyone to take 4 simple steps this Cybersecurity Awareness Month to be more cyber wise. Anyone can participate- individuals, home offices, businesses of any sizes, charities, not for profits.

The 4 steps are:

1. Update your devices regularly
2. Turn on multi- factor authentication (MFA)
3. Back up your important files
4. Use passphrases and password managers

The 4 steps may seem simple, but a large number of cybersecurity incidents stem from these 4 simple things.

Having dealt with several cyber security breaches, most cyber breaches I have been called to help can be attributed to one of the four steps. My family, friends, clients will often hear me nag them about taking some time to attend to these 4 steps.

Please take the time this month to tick off the above 4 basic steps to keep your IT system secure. A good number of security breaches are often due to a lapse in one of the above steps.

### The reasons why

#### 1. Update your devices regularly

Coding is not perfect and often flaws are uncovered. A common frustration I hear from many software developers/ engineers is that the software development process is too business like. Time is money after all. Meeting deadlines to push software through to the user is more important- and they can patch stuff up later in updates.

If you find a hole in your house that you found that mice are getting through, I guarantee you’ll be going straight to a hardware store to seal up the hole. Similarly, it’s important to patch up the holes to stop the cyber snooping mice from stealing your cheese (personal data and potentially your identity).

#### 2.Turn on multi- factor authentication (MFA)

A key defensive strategy that is simple and effective is to have many security checkpoints to stop intruders. Again, just like your house, you invest money in locks on your door, security screens, alarm systems, CCTV… all are multiple checkpoints to make it hard for thieves to enter.

Yes, I get lots of complaints about how annoying it is that you need to find your phone to enter a 6-digit code after you’ve entered your username and password. But you don’t find it hard that you’ve got to enter your alarm code, lock your main door and then lock your security gate.

MFA provides several rings of independent authorisation to prevent would-be intruders from accessing your technological systems. Trust me, it’s worth the few minutes to get your phone to enter in a code.

#### 3. Back up your important files

Backing up is another very simple but often overlooked aspect of staying safe. It’s essential for recovery after a cyber breach. With cyber incidents like crypto-lockers whereby your data is hijacked and locked up and the attackers demand a ridiculous amount of money to unlock your data (most don’t even give you the code to unlock even after paying them), it’s easier to delete everything and restore your precious files and photos from the back up. Even for other cyber breaches, restoring your data that was backed up prior to incident is a key part of getting you back on track.

#### 4. Use passphrases and password managers

Yes, even IT specialists and cyber security specialists hate passwords. Usernames and passwords can be brute forced (guessed) or even bought from the dark web. Scammers can see in real time (after conning you to download a virus) what you’re typing on your screen. Your job is to make it hard for them. We have covered this extensively in a previous blog post [Hacking your account Part 1: Username and passwords](/_posts/2024-01-20-hacking-your-account-part-1-username-and-passwords.md).
